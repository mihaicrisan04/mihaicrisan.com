an ecommerce store built around a grounded AI shopping advisor — my bachelor thesis at babes-bolyai university. the research question: how do you integrate an LLM into a shop so it's actually trustworthy? not a demo — it's deployed and running at [zalem.mihais-vps.xyz](https://zalem.mihais-vps.xyz).

## why

commercial AI shopping assistants have a documented trust problem. trade reports on Amazon's Rufus — 300M+ users — found it picks the right product ~32% of the time, quotes wrong prices in ~28% of answers, favors Amazon's own brand in 83% of comparisons, and fabricates review themes. confidently wrong, at scale.

classical recommenders have the opposite weakness: they narrow a huge catalog accurately but can never tell you *why*. zalem's answer is to combine them so each side only does what it's good at — and to shape the system so the model *can't* lie unnoticed:

- **reactive, not proactive** — browsing signals (dwell, scroll, review engagement) prepare context and surface a subtle "ask advisor" hint, but the AI never speaks uninvited
- **two-stage recommendations** — classical algorithms (co-occurrence, attribute similarity with MMR re-ranking, time-decayed trending) generate the candidates; the LLM only re-ranks and explains, never touches the raw catalog
- **hydration over generation** — the model outputs product *IDs*, the client hydrates them into live catalog data. every price, rating, and spec on screen is read from the database on every request, never from the model

## the advisor

a Convex agent with five read-only tools (product details, search, recommendations, cart contents, review summaries), streaming word-by-word into a sidebar. the model **selects and explains; it never sources a fact.**

every question gets fresh context assembled from the live session — the product you're on, its review themes, your cart, recently viewed — and a validation layer runs before anything renders: every cited product ID is verified, factual claims that can't be traced to a tool call are stripped, and claimed review themes are checked against the actual review corpus.

<ProjectImage src="https://ik.imagekit.io/mihaicrisan/projects/zalem/gallery/03-advisor-context.png" alt="advisor context assembled per question" caption="session state, rebuilt on every question — handed to the advisor with every message" />

## review summaries you can check

a nightly pipeline summarizes reviews into structured positives, negatives, and — the headline feature — **divided opinions**, where buyers genuinely disagree ("11 say the fit is secure, 5 say they work loose"). every extracted theme is validated twice: quotes are matched back to source reviews, and embedding similarity confirms each theme appears in at least two of them. anything that fails is dropped before it ships.

## does the grounding actually matter?

a controlled comparison: same model, same 34 questions, but with the catalog tools removed so it answers from memory:

- **specific price/rating claims**: 83 grounded vs **162 ungrounded** — cut off from the database, the model compensates by asserting twice as much
- **claims that fail the catalog check**: 48% vs **67%**
- **factuality**: 0.76 vs **0.53**

and the kicker: LLM judges *preferred* the ungrounded answers. fluency and truthfulness are different axes, and only one of them shows up in a demo.

the shipped config came out of a custom eval harness (Promptfoo + Convex-aware scorers) sweeping 8 configurations — model × reasoning effort × step budget × prompt variant — scoring groundedness, factuality, review-theme fidelity, and tool-call efficiency. the winner was the *cheapest* model: composite 0.807, ~$0.0003 per query, 4.2s p95. more reasoning actually scored worse, and groundedness held ≥ 0.89 across all 8 configs. **trust is an integration problem, not a model-capability problem.**

## honest limits

no user study yet, the catalog is ~200 seeded products, and it wasn't benchmarked against a standard product-search baseline. next: a within-subjects trust study, and fine-tuning a smaller model.

## stack notes

turborepo + bun monorepo: Next.js 16 storefront (React 19 with the compiler enabled), Convex backend (13 tables, vector + full-text indexes, nightly recommendation crons), a shared shadcn/ui design system, and the eval package. models routed through OpenRouter — swappable because the eval harness makes regressions measurable. deployed on a self-hosted VPS, with the Convex backend auto-deployed via GitHub Actions.
