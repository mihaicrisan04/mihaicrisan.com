a turn-based board game with an AI opponent, built as a 1st-year university project at UBB. two players take turns claiming cells on a grid; each claim blocks all 8 neighbouring cells. whoever can't move loses.

## why

the course had two goals stapled together — write something with a non-trivial architecture, and implement a game AI from scratch. obstruction is small enough to fit a semester and deep enough that a brute-force search is unplayable, which made it the right forcing function for both.

## how the AI works

the opponent uses **minimax with alpha-beta pruning**. the pruning is what makes it usable in practice: without it, even modest board sizes blow the search tree up to seconds-per-move. with it, the AI responds in well under a second and rarely loses to a human.

## architecture

a layered Python codebase, structured the way the course wanted us to internalise:

- `domain/` — `Game`, `Board`, `Player`, `AI` (pure logic, no IO)
- `ui/` — both a `pygame` GUI and a console renderer, swappable from `settings.properties`
- `services/` — wiring between them

the same domain runs untouched behind either UI, which is the whole point of the exercise. bug-fixing the AI never touched the renderer; swapping the renderer never touched the rules.
